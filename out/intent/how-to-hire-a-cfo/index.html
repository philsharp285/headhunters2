How to Hire a CFO in the UK

A practical guide to CFO executive search in the UK — salary benchmarks, search fees, timelines, and what to look for in a search firm.

The CFO is the CEO's most important partner and the person most directly responsible for the financial integrity, reporting quality, and capital allocation decision-making of the organisation. In regulated industries, the CFO is also typically an approved person under FCA or PRA frameworks, adding regulatory complexity to the appointment process. The search for a CFO is consistently one of the most active mandates in UK executive search, and one of the most common to be mishandled — either because the process is insufficiently rigorous, because the candidate specification is built around the past rather than the future, or because the compensation offer is out of step with a market that has moved significantly.

CFO profiles vary enormously by context. The CFO of a pre-IPO technology business needs entirely different characteristics from the CFO of a mature FTSE 250 industrial company. The CFO of a PE-backed portfolio company — focused on EBITDA management, working capital optimisation, and exit preparation — has a very different mandate from the CFO of a regulated bank, where capital adequacy, regulatory reporting, and stress testing are the dominant responsibilities. Building the right candidate specification requires an honest conversation about which CFO the organisation needs for the next three to five years, not simply what the previous CFO looked like.

In the current market, CFO search timelines are typically 12–16 weeks from briefing to accepted offer, with notice periods of three to six months adding materially to the calendar before the new appointment starts. Organisations that begin CFO searches with a sense of urgency — driven by an unexpected departure or a board-imposed timeline — consistently make worse appointments than those that begin the process early, with adequate time for thorough assessment and market coverage.

Key Considerations When Hiring a CFO

  1. Define whether you need a "controller CFO" (processes, compliance, reporting) or a "business partner CFO" (commercial, strategic, growth-oriented) — these are fundamentally different profiles and the market for each is distinct.

  2. Check regulatory requirements upfront: for FCA/PRA-regulated entities, confirm that the preferred candidate is willing to apply for and hold the required approvals before making an offer.

  3. Assess technical accounting depth as rigorously as commercial capability — CFOs who lack robust financial control expertise create material governance and audit risk regardless of their commercial strengths.

  4. Reference deeply with the CFO's previous CFOs or audit committee chairs, not just CEOs and colleagues — the most revealing references for a CFO come from those who scrutinised their financial reporting.

  5. Consider succession implications: does the appointment leave the organisation with a viable FD or VP Finance who could step up, or does the external CFO hire create a single point of leadership failure in the finance function?

Salary Range

£150,000–£500,000+ depending on sector, listed/private/PE context, and scale of finance function

Search Fee

27%–33% of agreed compensation, typically with a minimum of £40,000

Typical Timeline

12–16 weeks to accepted offer; 3–6 month notice periods are standard; total calendar 6–10 months

Why Use Executive Search for This Role?

Strong CFO candidates are among the most passively employed executives in the UK market. A CFO who is performing well, has the confidence of the CEO and audit committee, and is well-compensated has every reason to stay and almost no incentive to engage with unsolicited approaches from unknown recruiters. Reaching the best CFO candidates — those who are settled, performing, and not actively looking — requires a search firm with genuine relationships in the financial leadership market, the credibility to make a compelling first approach, and the sector knowledge to position the opportunity in a way that makes the disruption of a move genuinely worthwhile.
