Confidential Executive Search UK

A practical guide to Confidential Appointment executive search in the UK — salary benchmarks, search fees, timelines, and what to look for in a search firm.

Confidential executive search — the appointment of a senior executive through a search process that is invisible to the market, the incumbent, or both — is one of the most technically demanding services that executive search firms provide. It requires a different approach to candidate identification, a more controlled briefing process, and a level of discretion in candidate communication that standard search methodology does not automatically deliver. Done well, a confidential search produces the same quality of outcome as any other retained engagement. Done poorly, it creates reputational damage, legal risk, and the kind of market visibility that a confidential process was specifically designed to avoid.

The most common scenarios requiring confidential executive search are: replacement of a sitting executive who does not know they are being replaced, or who is underperforming but whose departure has not been announced; succession to a role where the announcement of a search would signal an unplanned or sensitive leadership change; new role creation that the organisation is not ready to announce publicly; and searches where the hiring organisation's identity needs to be protected from the candidate market during the early stages of the process.

Each of these scenarios requires specific process adaptations. The candidate approach cannot identify the client organisation until the candidate has demonstrated genuine interest and agreed to confidentiality. Reference-taking must be managed with particular care — checking references with a candidate's current employer before an offer is made is standard practice in open searches but completely prohibited in confidential ones. And the internal process — briefing, approvals, decision-making — must be managed with a smaller group of stakeholders than is typical, reducing the risk of inadvertent disclosure.

Key Considerations When Hiring a Confidential Appointment

  1. Define exactly what "confidential" means for this specific search: is the client identity confidential, the role confidential, the fact that a search is underway confidential, or all three?

  2. Agree a protocol for candidate communication that specifies exactly what information is shared at each stage, and when the client organisation is identified to candidates who have expressed genuine interest.

  3. Limit the internal briefing group to the minimum necessary — every additional person who knows a confidential search is underway increases the risk of inadvertent disclosure.

  4. Do not attempt to reference candidates before an offer is made: references in a confidential search are conducted post-offer, with the candidate's knowledge and consent.

  5. Brief the search firm on what to say if a contacted candidate knows the sitting incumbent — the search firm needs a protocol for managing this situation without creating an awkward disclosure.

Salary Range

Varies by role; confidential searches span the full range of executive compensation levels

Search Fee

28%–35% of agreed compensation; some firms apply a 2%–5% premium for fully confidential mandates reflecting additional process complexity

Typical Timeline

12–18 weeks for fully confidential CEO/C-suite searches; candidate approach and disclosure process adds 2–4 weeks versus standard searches

Why Use Executive Search for This Role?

Confidential executive search cannot be conducted effectively through advertising, LinkedIn, or conventional recruitment agencies. The moment a role appears on any public channel, the confidentiality of the search is compromised. Retained executive search firms with the processes, relationship networks, and operational discipline to conduct fully confidential mandates are the only appropriate vehicle for sensitive appointments. The premium in fee and time that confidential search requires is modest relative to the reputational, relational, and operational cost of a confidential process that becomes visible before it should.
