How to Hire a CEO in the UK

A practical guide to CEO executive search in the UK — salary benchmarks, search fees, timelines, and what to look for in a search firm.

Hiring a CEO is the most consequential appointment a board makes. The decision affects every dimension of organisational performance, culture, and strategic direction — and the consequences of getting it wrong, at a time and cost that is very difficult to absorb, are well documented. A CEO appointment that fails within 18 months of hire — which occurs in roughly 25–30% of cases when the process is inadequately rigorous — costs the organisation between 5 and 10 times the CEO's annual base salary in direct and indirect costs.

The CEO search process is distinct from any other senior appointment in several important respects. The candidate pool is smaller, more visible, and more carefully managed. The assessment requirements are higher. The board involvement is more intensive. The timelines — particularly for listed or regulated organisations — are longer. And the risk of counter-offers, candidate withdrawal, and process failure is higher than for any other role. These characteristics mean that the CEO search process requires specific expertise, not simply a standard executive search methodology applied to a more senior role.

In the UK, the most effective CEO appointments combine three elements: a rigorously maintained succession plan that identifies internal candidates and their development gaps; an external market intelligence view that gives the board a current picture of the external candidate landscape; and, when a search is triggered, a retained search firm with genuine sector relationships, board-level assessment capability, and the experience to manage the process from briefing through to the candidate's first day in post.

Key Considerations When Hiring a CEO

  1. Define the strategic mandate first — the CEO profile must be derived from the next three to five years of organisational strategy, not from the profile of the previous CEO.

  2. Assess internal candidates honestly, with independent external assessment, before deciding the search must go external — strong internal candidates are significantly less expensive and carry lower integration risk.

  3. Brief a single retained search firm with exclusive commitment — multiple agencies on a CEO search produce poor quality and compromise confidentiality.

  4. Allocate adequate time: 16–20 weeks from briefing to appointment is realistic for C-suite searches; planning around 12 weeks creates execution pressure that impairs decision quality.

  5. Design the board process carefully — who is involved in assessment, at what stages, with what mandate — before the search begins, not once candidates are being presented.

Salary Range

£200,000–£700,000+ depending on sector, scale, and PE/listed/private context

Search Fee

28%–35% of agreed compensation (base + bonus), with a minimum of £50,000

Typical Timeline

16–20 weeks from briefing to accepted offer; allow an additional 3–6 months for notice periods

Why Use Executive Search for This Role?

The most capable CEO candidates are, without exception, passive. They are leading organisations, delivering results, and are not browsing CEO job boards. Reaching them requires a search firm with genuine senior relationships, the credibility to make a compelling approach on behalf of a client, and the experience to manage the complex, multi-stage assessment and offer process that CEO appointments require. Board nominations committee members who attempt to manage CEO searches without a specialist firm — relying on their own networks and introductions — consistently produce smaller candidate pools, shallower assessment, and higher rates of appointment failure. The cost of a poor CEO appointment dwarfs the cost of the search fee by an order of magnitude.
