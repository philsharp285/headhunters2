How to Appoint a Board Chair in the UK

UK salary benchmarks, search fees, timelines, and what to look for in a headhunter for this role.

The appointment of a Chair is the most significant governance decision a board makes. The Chair sets board culture, manages board dynamics, holds the CEO accountable, represents the company to major stakeholders, and guides the organisation through its most consequential decisions. A poor Chair appointment affects every aspect of governance and, over time, strategic performance.

The Chair's relationship with the CEO is the most important bilateral relationship in the governance of any organisation. It must combine support and challenge in careful balance — a Chair who is too deferential fails in the accountability role; a Chair who micromanages undermines executive authority and CEO effectiveness. Getting this relationship right starts with the appointment.

In listed companies, the Chair appointment is subject to significant regulatory and shareholder scrutiny. Independence requirements, tenure guidelines, and committee chair responsibilities are all governed by the UK Corporate Governance Code. In private and PE-backed businesses, the Chair role is more flexible but no less important — and the expectations of investors must be explicitly understood before the brief is written.

Salary Range

£40,000–£120,000 (Chair fee) depending on organisation size and time commitment

Search Fee

Fixed fee or 25–33% of first-year Chair fee

Typical Timeline

10–16 weeks from briefing to appointment (often longer for listed company Chairs)

Key Considerations When Hiring a Board Chair

  1. Define the specific phase the company is in and what the Chair must prioritise — governance improvement, stakeholder management, a transaction, or a leadership transition.

  2. Clarify the Chair/CEO relationship model and ensure the incoming CEO (if concurrent) has input into the Chair selection process where appropriate.

  3. Establish time commitment expectations honestly — Chair roles at complex organisations frequently require more time than the advertised commitment suggests.

  4. Consider listed company governance requirements if applicable — independence status, tenure, and committee responsibilities must all comply with the UK Corporate Governance Code.

  5. Define the succession context — is this a long-term appointment or a specific phase appointment with a defined transition?

Why Use Executive Search for This Role?

Chair candidates are entirely passive. The best Chairs are serving on other boards, are well-compensated, and are highly selective about new commitments. They will not respond to advertisements. The headhunter's role is to identify appropriate candidates with the right combination of independence, sector experience, and governance track record; to approach them compellingly; and to manage the governance process through to appointment.
